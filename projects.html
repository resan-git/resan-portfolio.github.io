Pressure-Monitored Non-Pneumatic Anti-Shock Garment (NASG)

Biomedical Device Design | Level 3 Term 1

Problem: Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH) accounts for a significant portion of maternal mortality in low-resource settings. Conventional non-pneumatic anti-shock garments are effective for temporary stabilization but lack mechanisms for monitoring and controlling applied pressure, raising concerns about safety and effectiveness.

Approach: We designed an enhanced NASG incorporating real-time pressure monitoring and feedback. Force Sensitive Resistors (FSRs) were embedded into the garment and interfaced with an Arduino Nano to continuously measure applied pressure. A customizable lacing mechanism enabled controlled, segment-wise pressure application using durable Neoprene fabric and stainless steel buckles.

Key Engineering Innovations:

  • Real-time pressure acquisition using FSR sensors
  • Automated buzzer-based alarm for unsafe pressure levels
  • Neoprene-based garment design to prevent permanent deformation
  • Improved mechanical reliability using stainless steel buckles

Calibration & Validation: The system was calibrated using a standard blood pressure cuff and human subjects. ADC thresholds corresponding to 30–50 mmHg were established. Performance was validated using a simulated limb model and human testing, confirming stable pressure maintenance without discomfort.

Outcome: The prototype successfully maintained an optimized pressure range (30–50 mmHg), demonstrated effective fluid redistribution toward the core, and showed no significant material degradation over time.

Tools & Technologies: Arduino Nano, Force Sensitive Resistors (FSR), Neoprene fabric, stainless steel mechanical components, basic signal conditioning circuits.

Future Scope: Incorporating multiple sensors for spatial averaging, rechargeable power systems, and fully automated pressure regulation using closed-loop control.

Low-Cost Ankle Joint Exoskeleton for Foot-Drop Assistance

Assistive Biomechanical System | Level 3 Term 2

Problem: Foot drop is a gait disorder characterized by impaired dorsiflexion, significantly increasing fall risk and limiting mobility. Existing assistive exoskeleton solutions are often expensive and inaccessible, particularly in low-resource settings.

Approach: We designed a low-cost, sensor-driven ankle exoskeleton capable of detecting gait phases and providing real-time mechanical assistance. The system uses force-sensitive resistors (FSRs) to detect foot-ground interaction and triggers controlled ankle motion through a high-torque servo-driven actuation mechanism.

Key Engineering Innovations:

  • Cost-efficient assistive design (~USD 180 total system cost)
  • Chain-and-sprocket transmission driven by a 35 kg digital servo motor
  • Hybrid structural design using aluminum, stainless steel, and 3D-printed PLA
  • Real-time gait phase detection using FSR-based feedback

System Architecture: An Arduino Uno microcontroller processes pressure signals from FSR sensors embedded in the footplate. Based on calibrated thresholds, the controller activates the servo motor during appropriate gait phases to assist ankle dorsiflexion and stabilize foot positioning.

Validation & Testing: Calibration experiments were conducted on multiple healthy subjects to tune sensor thresholds and verify timing accuracy. Iterative mechanical refinements, including CNC-cut aluminum connectors and joint lubrication, improved durability and reduced friction.

Outcome: The final prototype demonstrated effective real-time assistance, portable battery-powered operation, and adjustable support levels. The system highlights the feasibility of building functional assistive exoskeletons using low-cost components.

Tools & Technologies: Arduino Uno, Force Sensitive Resistors (FSR), digital servo motor (35 kg), aluminum alloy frame, stainless steel sprockets, 3D-printed PLA components, Li-ion battery system.

Limitations & Future Scope: Future work includes reducing system weight using advanced materials, optimizing battery performance, integrating wireless communication, and conducting clinical trials with foot-drop patients to refine control strategies.

ECG & EMG Acquisition System with Mobile App Interface

Biomedical Signal Processing & PCB Design | Level 3 Term 1

Problem: Biopotential signals such as ECG and EMG have very low amplitudes and are highly susceptible to motion artifacts and electromagnetic interference, making accurate acquisition and interpretation challenging in portable systems.

Approach: We designed two optimized signal acquisition chains for ECG and EMG, integrating precision amplification, multi-stage filtering, embedded processing, and wireless data transmission to a mobile application for real-time monitoring.

Key Engineering Innovations:

  • Low-noise signal amplification using AD620 instrumentation amplifier
  • Custom gain design tailored for ECG (~626) and EMG (~278.78)
  • 5th-order high-pass and low-pass filters for band-limiting
  • Passive twin-T notch filter for 50/60 Hz powerline noise suppression

Signal Processing & Embedded Logic: An Arduino Uno was programmed to perform peak detection on ECG signals for heart rate estimation and threshold-based analysis on EMG signals to distinguish between muscle contraction and relaxation events.

Simulation & Implementation: Circuit designs were validated in TinaTI through transient and frequency-domain analyses before PCB implementation. While simulations showed excellent bandwidth conformity, hardware testing revealed practical PCB challenges such as soldering faults and short circuits.

Outcome: The system demonstrated successful real-time heart rate tracking and muscle activity detection, wirelessly transmitting processed data to a mobile interface via Bluetooth.

Tools & Technologies: AD620 instrumentation amplifier, Arduino Uno, HC-05 Bluetooth module, TinaTI simulation, PCB design and fabrication, ECG & EMG surface electrodes.

Future Scope: Improving PCB layout robustness, integrating ECG and EMG modes into a single compact circuit, and enhancing mechanical housing for reliable mobile use.

Pneumonia Detection from Chest X-ray Images

Deep Learning & Medical Image Analysis | Undergraduate Project

Problem: Manual interpretation of chest X-ray images is time-consuming and prone to inter-observer variability. An automated screening system can assist clinicians by rapidly identifying potential pneumonia cases.

Approach: We developed a convolutional neural network (CNN)-based binary classification system to detect pneumonia from chest X-ray images. The pipeline includes image preprocessing, supervised model training, and performance evaluation using standard classification metrics.

Methodology:

  • Preprocessing and normalization of chest X-ray images
  • CNN-based binary classification (Normal vs Pneumonia)
  • Model evaluation using accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score

Dataset: Chest X-ray Images (Pneumonia) dataset sourced from Kaggle. Only a subset of sample images is included in the repository for demonstration purposes.

Outcome: The trained model demonstrated the feasibility of automated pneumonia detection from X-ray images and produced reasonable classification results on sample test images.

Limitations: The model was trained on a relatively small dataset, lacks clinical validation, and its performance is sensitive to image quality variations.

Tools & Technologies: Python, TensorFlow/Keras, CNN architectures, OpenCV, Kaggle dataset, GitHub-based version control.

Future Scope: Incorporating transfer learning models (e.g., ResNet, EfficientNet), applying data augmentation for improved robustness, and extending the system to multi-class lung disease classification.

Disclaimer: This project is intended strictly for academic demonstration and is not suitable for clinical use.

Interactive Thermal Image Analysis Tool Using Color-to-Temperature Mapping

Medical Imaging & GUI Development | Imaging Course (Level 4 Term 1)

Problem: Infrared (IR) thermal cameras visualize temperature distributions using color-coded heatmaps. However, interpreting exact temperature values from these images often requires manual reference to color bars, which is inefficient and prone to error during analysis.

Approach: We developed an interactive MATLAB-based graphical user interface (GUI) that allows users to click on any pixel within a thermal image and instantly retrieve the corresponding temperature value by mapping RGB intensities to a predefined thermal colormap.

Methodology:

  • Extracted the reference thermal colormap provided by the IR camera system
  • Sliced the colormap into discrete RGB values associated with known temperatures
  • Stored RGB–temperature pairs in an indexed array
  • Computed Euclidean distance between clicked pixel RGB values and reference colormap
  • Assigned the temperature corresponding to the minimum distance match

GUI Functionality: The interface enables users to load thermal images, interactively select arbitrary locations using mouse clicks, and display the estimated temperature value at the selected region in real time.

Outcome: The system successfully translated color-coded thermal information into quantitative temperature readings, enabling intuitive and efficient thermal analysis without requiring manual interpretation of color bars.

Tools & Technologies: MATLAB, image processing toolbox, GUI development, RGB color space analysis, infrared thermal imaging data.

Extensions & Future Scope: Adding region-based averaging, automated hotspot detection, improved interpolation across color bands, and real-time video stream processing from IR cameras.
