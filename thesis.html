Undergraduate Thesis

Skin Tone–Aware Generation of Dermatological Images Using GAN-Based Models

B.Sc. in Biomedical Engineering | Bangladesh University of Engineering and Technology (BUET)

This thesis investigates the problem of racial and skin-tone bias in dermatological image datasets and proposes a generative deep learning framework to synthesize skin lesion images across diverse Fitzpatrick skin tones while preserving lesion-specific characteristics.

Research Motivation

Most publicly available dermatology datasets are heavily biased toward lighter skin tones, resulting in reduced diagnostic performance for darker skin populations. This imbalance limits the fairness and generalizability of AI-based diagnostic systems.

The goal of this thesis is to address this imbalance by generating skin-tone–diverse lesion images that preserve medically relevant lesion features while varying only the surrounding skin tone.

Methodology

  • Used Pix2Pix and CycleGAN architectures for image-to-image translation
  • Generated semantic maps encoding lesion region and target skin tone
  • Applied Fitzpatrick skin tone labeling (1–6) for controlled generation
  • Integrated a pretrained skin tone classifier to enforce tone consistency
  • Performed background removal and hair artifact reduction for robustness

Key Contributions

  • Proposed a controllable image generation framework for six Fitzpatrick skin tones
  • Preserved lesion morphology while varying skin tone appearance
  • Introduced classifier-guided loss for tone-aware generation
  • Analyzed the impact of synthetic data on downstream disease classification

Results & Observations

The proposed model successfully generated skin lesion images across different skin tones. However, expert clinical feedback indicated that while skin tone variation was achieved, certain disease-specific visual features were not always preserved with sufficient fidelity.

This highlights the limitations of current GAN-based approaches in medical image synthesis and underscores the importance of clinician-in-the-loop evaluation.

Thesis Status

This work was completed as part of the undergraduate thesis requirement. A formal thesis defense presentation was conducted, and the work is currently being refined for potential extension in future graduate research.
